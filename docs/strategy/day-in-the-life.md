# Day in the Life — Mike's Tuesday

**Purpose**: This document is the emotional and operational spine of Serviceos.
It is the source of truth for the pitch deck, the homepage, the PRD, and the
customer interview script. Every feature we ship must move a moment from the
left column to the right column. Every feature that does not, doesn't ship.

If we drift, we come back here.

---

## The pitch in one sentence

> **You learned the trade. We run the business.**
> AI answers your phone, books your jobs, sends your estimates, and chases your
> invoices. You approve what matters in 30 seconds a day.
>
> Built for the shop with 2 trucks and no office.

---

## The customer

**Mike Rivera, 38.** Owner of M&R Mechanical, a 2-truck HVAC shop in Phoenix.
Started the company four years ago after twelve years as a tech at a bigger
outfit. Married, two kids (7 and 4). Wife works full-time as a nurse.

One employee: **Carlos**, his cousin, a solid tech but won't touch paperwork.

Mike's current stack:
- Cell phone (his personal number is also the business number)
- Google Calendar
- Housecall Pro Starter ($49/mo) for invoicing
- QuickBooks Self-Employed for accounting
- A notebook in the truck for parts and estimates
- His wife, on weekends, for "the books"

Revenue: ~$680k last year. He wants to add a third truck but can't — there's
no time to hire, train, or dispatch a new person. He's the bottleneck and he
knows it.

His real job title, if he's honest, is **dispatcher, CSR, estimator,
bookkeeper, collections agent, marketing manager, and HVAC technician.** Only
the last one is the one he wanted.

## What we are *not* doing for Mike

Scope discipline matters. This pitch will draw requests we have to say no to.

**In scope (now):**
- Inbound call answering and booking
- SMS triage and replies in the shop's voice
- Estimate drafting from call recordings + history
- Invoice drafting from completed jobs + tech notes
- Payment link generation and unpaid-invoice follow-up
- Schedule visibility and customer reminders
- End-of-day digest
- Audit trail of every AI action

**Later (post product-market fit):**
- Outbound marketing (review requests, win-back campaigns, seasonal tune-up reminders)
- Maintenance agreement management
- Parts ordering from suppliers
- Multi-location dispatch optimization
- Recruiting / hiring assistance
- Equipment lifecycle tracking from photos

**Never (not our job):**
- Tax filing
- Payroll calculation (we surface hours; QuickBooks/Gusto pays them)
- Legal advice
- Vendor price negotiation
- HR / firing decisions
- Anything that requires Mike to log into a separate dashboard for >30 seconds

## What this forces on the product

If we commit to Mike's Tuesday, the following are no longer up for debate:

1. **Primary interface is SMS.** A web app exists for audit and configuration,
   but no daily action requires opening it. If Mike has to open the app to do
   his job, we failed.

2. **End-of-day digest is the dashboard.** No real-time charts. No "you've
   booked 47% more jobs this quarter." A 6pm text summary respects the
   customer's attention.

3. **One-tap approvals.** Every proposal is a single SMS with **Approve / Edit
   / Reject**. Edits accept dictation. No forms.

4. **Emergency intent overrides automation.** When a call carries urgency
   signals (no AC + heat warning + vulnerable household), the AI dials Mike
   rather than trying to book. Mode-aware: if Mike is unavailable, partner
   referral fallback.

5. **Memory of every customer, job, and quote, accessible by voice.** *"What
   did we quote the Patel job?"* returns an answer in seconds. This is a
   first-class feature, not a search box.

6. **Auto-generated artifacts from call recordings.** Estimates and invoices
   are drafted from the transcript + history, not from Mike typing into a
   form. Form-filling is the failure mode we exist to eliminate.

7. **Brand voice is configurable, then locked.** Mike sets the tone once
   (friendly, no jargon, "we'll get to you as soon as we can"). Everything
   Serviceos says — calls, texts, invoices, follow-ups — sounds like Mike's
   shop.

8. **No feature ships that adds admin work to Mike's day.** The litmus test.

## How to use this document

- **Engineering**: When you're about to build a feature, find where in Mike's
  day it shows up. If it doesn't show up, it's the wrong feature.
- **Design**: Every screen and SMS should be testable against *"would Mike,
  in the attic, on a hot day, with gloves on, find this useful in 5 seconds?"*
- **Marketing**: The pitch deck opens with the without-column. Page 2 is the
  with-column. Page 3 is the pricing. That's the deck.
- **Sales**: Read the without-column to a prospect. Stop after each row. Ask
  *"is this you?"* You'll know in three minutes whether they're an ICP.
- **Customer research**: Read the without-column to 5 owner-operators. Ask
  what's wrong, what's missing, what's the worst part. Their answers become
  the next version of this document.

This document is versioned. When it changes, the product changes.
